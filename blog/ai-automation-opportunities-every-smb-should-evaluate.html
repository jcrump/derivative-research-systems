AI Automation Opportunities Every SMB Should Evaluate

Where to start, what to skip, and how to measure whether it's working.

JC
Jeff Crump
Founder & Principal Architect, DRS
December 1, 2025
4 min read

The gap between SMBs that believe AI would help their business and SMBs that have actually deployed it is significant — and it is not a technology gap. It is a prioritization gap. Most SMB leaders understand that AI has potential. Far fewer have a clear framework for deciding where to start.

This post gives you that framework: three categories of automation that consistently deliver the highest return for the lowest implementation complexity in a 10–200 person company.

Category 1: Document Processing and Extraction

If your team reads documents to extract specific information — invoices, contracts, applications, support tickets, compliance filings — that is a strong automation candidate. The pattern is consistent: a human reads a document, finds specific fields or triggers, and enters the information into another system or makes a decision based on it.

Modern AI document processing can handle the extraction step with accuracy rates that exceed manual data entry for structured and semi-structured documents. The ROI calculation is straightforward: how many hours per week does your team spend reading to extract, multiplied by your fully-loaded labor cost, minus the implementation and operational cost of the automated system. For most SMBs processing more than 50 documents per week, the payback period is under six months.

Category 2: Customer Communication Triage and Drafting

Customer communications — support tickets, quote requests, follow-up emails, FAQ responses — follow predictable patterns. A trained system can classify incoming requests by type, route them to the right team or individual, and draft a response that a human reviews and sends. The human is not removed from the process. Their time is redirected from reading and writing to reviewing and approving.

SMBs that have implemented AI-assisted customer communication report 40–60% reductions in first-response time and 2–3 hours per week recovered per customer-facing employee. The tool handles the predictable. The human handles the judgment calls.

Category 3: Internal Reporting and Data Assembly

Most SMBs have a recurring reporting process that follows the same pattern every week or month: pull data from System A, pull data from System B, reconcile them, format the result, and distribute it. This process exists because the systems do not talk to each other, and a human is acting as the integration layer.

Automating this is often faster and cheaper than people expect. If your source systems have APIs — and most modern SaaS tools do — the assembly process can be automated in days, not months. The result is a report that is more current, more consistent, and produced without the four hours of manual work it currently requires.

What to Skip for Now

Avoid automating complex judgment calls, highly variable customer interactions, or any process where the cost of an error is asymmetric. AI tools perform best in high-volume, pattern-driven workflows where human review remains in the loop. The goal in the first 90 days is to find a process where automation clearly wins — not to find the most ambitious possible application.

How to Know If It's Working

Before you automate anything, measure the baseline. Time spent per week on the target process, error rate, and the cost of errors. Measure again at 30, 60, and 90 days. If the automation is working, the numbers improve. If they do not, you adjust. This discipline — measure before, measure after, decide based on evidence — is what separates successful AI adoption from expensive experimentation.
